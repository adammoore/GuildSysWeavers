# When teams use the same term for different things

## Context

Multiple teams, systems, or organisations use the same word or phrase — but mean different things by it. This is not immediately obvious because the shared vocabulary creates an illusion of alignment.

## Problem

Shared terminology without shared meaning is one of the most common and insidious sources of integration failure. It causes data mismatches, process errors, miscommunication, and failed projects — all while everyone believes they are talking about the same thing.

## Forces

- Language evolves independently within teams, shaped by local context and history
- The longer a term has been used, the less likely anyone is to question its meaning
- Cross-team communication assumes alignment because the words match
- Data dictionaries, when they exist, often define structure (type, format) but not meaning
- Discovering semantic divergence requires conversations that cross team boundaries — conversations that may not happen naturally

## Warning signs

- Integration projects encounter unexpected data mismatches despite seemingly aligned schemas
- Cross-team meetings produce agreement that later turns out to be based on different assumptions
- The same report generated by different teams shows different numbers
- Users report that data from another system "doesn't look right"
- Attempts at standardisation reveal deep disagreements about what terms actually mean

## Anti-patterns / common bad fixes

- **Mandating a single definition without buy-in** — imposing a definition that does not reflect how the term is actually used creates compliance without understanding
- **Building mappings between systems without resolving the semantic difference** — the mapping may be technically correct but semantically misleading
- **Assuming the data model is the source of truth** — data models reflect design decisions, not necessarily operational meaning
- **Ignoring the divergence** because it seems minor — small semantic gaps produce large downstream errors

## Better approaches

- **Surface the divergence explicitly.** The most valuable step is often simply making visible that the same term means different things. This requires cross-team conversation.
- **Create a cross-system glossary** that documents how each term is used in each context, without prematurely forcing agreement.
- **Accept multiple meanings where they serve legitimate purposes.** Not every semantic difference needs to be resolved — some reflect real differences in context. The goal is awareness, not uniformity.
- **Resolve the divergence at integration points.** Where data crosses boundaries, be explicit about which definition is being used and what translation is required.
- **Involve people who use the data, not just people who manage it.** Operational users often understand the meaning of terms better than system administrators or data architects.

## Example scenario

Two departments in a government agency share a "case status" field. In Department A, "closed" means the investigation is complete. In Department B, "closed" means the case has been archived after a retention period. When the departments integrate their case management systems, hundreds of Department A cases appear to be archived in Department B's view, triggering an audit. A Systems Weaver identifies the semantic divergence, creates a mapping that preserves both meanings, and establishes a shared status vocabulary for the integration layer that is distinct from either department's internal usage.
