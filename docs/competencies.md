This framework describes the core competency domains for Systems Weaving practice. It is a first-pass map intended to make visible the skills this work actually requires — many of which are not captured by existing role definitions or technical skill inventories.

This is a living document. Contributions and refinements are welcome.

## Competency domains

### 1. Cross-system diagnosis

The ability to identify where and why things break across system boundaries — not within a single system, but at the joins between them.

**Includes:**
- Tracing failures across multiple systems and ownership boundaries
- Identifying root causes that span technical and organisational domains
- Recognising when a "system problem" is actually a boundary problem
- Distinguishing between component failures and integration failures

### 2. Semantic translation

The ability to recognise when different systems, teams, or processes use different terms or structures for the same underlying concept — or the same term for different concepts.

**Includes:**
- Mapping data meanings across systems with different schemas or vocabularies
- Translating between technical and business language
- Identifying where apparent alignment masks real divergence
- Building shared vocabularies across team boundaries

### 3. Workflow analysis

The ability to understand how work actually flows through an organisation, as distinct from how it is documented or designed to flow.

**Includes:**
- Mapping actual (not theoretical) process flows
- Identifying where workflows depend on informal practices or individual knowledge
- Recognising when formal process documentation has diverged from reality
- Understanding the human and institutional factors that shape how work moves

### 4. Interoperability thinking

The ability to think across technology boundaries about how systems can and should exchange information, state, and control.

**Includes:**
- Evaluating integration approaches (APIs, events, shared databases, manual bridges)
- Understanding the trade-offs between tight and loose coupling
- Designing connections that are resilient to change in either connected system
- Recognising when technical interoperability requires organisational agreement

### 5. Hidden dependency mapping

The ability to discover and document dependencies that are not formally recorded — the invisible connections that determine whether systems work or fail.

**Includes:**
- Identifying undocumented data flows between systems
- Recognising implicit sequencing requirements in processes
- Discovering where system behaviour depends on unwritten conventions
- Mapping the "shadow architecture" of workarounds and manual processes

### 6. Handoff design

The ability to design and improve the points where responsibility, data, or process control transfers from one team, system, or context to another.

**Includes:**
- Identifying where handoffs are unclear, incomplete, or missing
- Designing explicit handoff protocols that preserve necessary context
- Reducing information loss at transfer points
- Balancing handoff formality with operational speed

### 7. Socio-technical mediation

The ability to work effectively at the intersection of technical systems and human organisations — understanding that most infrastructure problems are socio-technical problems.

**Includes:**
- Facilitating agreement between teams with different priorities and vocabularies
- Navigating organisational politics to achieve technical outcomes
- Recognising when a technical problem requires an organisational solution (and vice versa)
- Building working relationships across team and institutional boundaries

### 8. Resilience under partial information

The ability to make sound decisions and take effective action when complete information is unavailable — which is the normal condition for cross-boundary work.

**Includes:**
- Working effectively with incomplete documentation
- Making reasonable inferences from limited evidence
- Knowing when to act on best-available information and when to seek more
- Maintaining situational awareness across multiple partially-understood systems

### 9. Operational sense-making

The ability to construct a coherent understanding of how a complex operational environment actually works, from fragmentary and sometimes contradictory sources.

**Includes:**
- Building mental models of complex multi-system environments
- Synthesising information from documentation, people, system behaviour, and institutional context
- Recognising when operational reality has diverged from architectural intent
- Communicating complex system states clearly to different audiences

### 10. Documentation of glue work

The ability to document cross-boundary knowledge — the connections, dependencies, workarounds, and context that typically exist only in experienced practitioners' heads.

**Includes:**
- Writing useful integration documentation (not just component documentation)
- Creating diagrams that show relationships and flows, not just boxes
- Documenting decisions, trade-offs, and the reasons for non-obvious choices
- Making tacit knowledge explicit and transferable

## Using this framework

This competency framework is descriptive, not prescriptive. It aims to name skills that practitioners already use, so they can be discussed, developed, and recognised.

It is **not** intended as:
- A checklist for hiring (no one person will be strong in all domains)
- A certification requirement
- A ranking or scoring system

It **is** intended to help practitioners:
- Articulate what they do
- Identify areas for development
- Understand the breadth of the craft
- Communicate their value to organisations and colleagues

See also: [Recognition pathways](recognition) for how demonstrated competence is acknowledged within the guild.
