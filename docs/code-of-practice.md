This code describes how Systems Weavers should approach their work responsibly and ethically. It is not a set of rules for enforcement, but a shared understanding of what responsible practice looks like in cross-boundary infrastructure work.

## Principles

### 1. Make the invisible visible

Systems Weavers often hold knowledge that exists nowhere else — about dependencies, handoffs, workarounds, and failure modes. Responsible practice means documenting this knowledge rather than hoarding it, so that systems do not depend on any single person's memory.

### 2. Respect existing systems and the people who built them

Cross-boundary work requires entering spaces owned by other teams and touching systems built by other people. Approach these with respect. Understand before you change. Ask before you assume.

### 3. Be honest about complexity

Resist pressure to oversimplify. If a system is fragile, say so. If a proposed integration will create hidden dependencies, name them. If a timeline is unrealistic given the actual state of the infrastructure, make that clear. The value of a Systems Weaver often lies precisely in telling the truth about how things actually work.

### 4. Build for the team that follows you

The connections you create should be understandable and maintainable by others. Avoid building solutions that only you can operate. Document your joins. Explain your workarounds. Make your knowledge transferable.

### 5. Acknowledge the limits of your knowledge

Working across many systems means you are rarely the deepest expert in any one of them. Know when to defer to specialists. Be clear about what you understand well and what you are inferring. Cross-boundary work is powerful precisely because it combines breadth with honest acknowledgement of depth.

### 6. Consider the human consequences

Systems serve people. When connecting or modifying workflows, consider the impact on the people who use them — not just the technical elegance of the solution. Changes to handoffs, data flows, and process boundaries affect working conditions, responsibilities, and sometimes people's jobs.

### 7. Do not create unnecessary dependencies on yourself

It can be tempting — and it can feel like job security — to become the only person who understands how things fit together. Resist this. The goal is to build institutional capability, not personal indispensability. A well-documented system that others can maintain is a better outcome than a brilliant solution only you can operate.

### 8. Share what you learn

The guild exists because this knowledge has historically been tacit and invisible. When you encounter a pattern, solve a boundary problem, or discover a failure mode, consider whether it can be shared — as a pattern, case study, or contribution to the guild's knowledge commons.

## Applying these principles

These principles are guides, not absolutes. Real-world systems work involves trade-offs, time pressure, and imperfect information. The code of practice does not demand perfection — it asks practitioners to hold these values in mind as they navigate the messy reality of cross-boundary work.
