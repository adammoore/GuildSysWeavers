This glossary defines terms used within the guild and related adjacent labels. It aims to provide clear language for a kind of work that has historically lacked consistent professional vocabulary.

## Preferred term

### Systems Weaver

The guild's primary term for this practice and its practitioners.

**Definition:** A practitioner who connects systems, teams, data, workflows and institutional realities across boundaries where formal ownership is fragmented or absent.

**Why this term:** "Systems" reflects the inherently cross-system nature of the work. "Weaver" captures the skill of joining, interlacing, and creating coherent fabric from disparate threads. The term is broad enough to encompass diverse contexts and dignified enough to serve as a professional identity.

**Use this when:** referring to the practice in professional, public-facing, or formal contexts.

## Related terms

These terms describe overlapping or adjacent practices. Some are used within the guild as complementary descriptions; others are informal labels from wider industry usage.

### Infrastructure Generalist

A practitioner with broad operational knowledge across multiple infrastructure domains, technologies, and platforms.

**Relationship to Systems Weaver:** Overlapping but narrower. An Infrastructure Generalist emphasises breadth of technical knowledge; a Systems Weaver emphasises the practice of connecting across boundaries. Many Systems Weavers are Infrastructure Generalists, but the weaving craft includes organisational and semantic dimensions beyond infrastructure.

### Workflow Stitcher

A practitioner focused on connecting and repairing the seams between processes, particularly where workflows cross team or system boundaries.

**Relationship to Systems Weaver:** A more specific description that highlights the process-repair dimension of the work. Useful when the primary challenge is workflow continuity rather than technical integration.

### Boundary Architect

A practitioner who designs or redesigns the interfaces and handoffs between systems, teams, or organisational units.

**Relationship to Systems Weaver:** Implies more design authority and formal influence than many Systems Weavers are granted. Useful in contexts where the practitioner has a recognised design or architecture role. May describe the aspiration of what Systems Weavers should be empowered to do.

### Technical Plumber

An informal, internally reclaimed label for the practitioner who connects the pipes between systems — often unglamorously, often under time pressure, often without formal recognition.

**Relationship to Systems Weaver:** Captures the practical, hands-on, sometimes unglamorous reality of the work. Useful as an internal or informal term of solidarity. Not recommended for public-facing professional use, as it can undersell the sophistication of the craft.

*Note: "Technical plumber" is treated within the guild as a reclaimed metaphor — acknowledged with respect but not used as the primary label.*

### Integration Generalist

A practitioner who works across multiple integration scenarios, technologies, and patterns without specialising in a single integration platform or approach.

**Relationship to Systems Weaver:** Focuses on the technical integration dimension. A Systems Weaver's scope typically extends beyond technical integration to include organisational, semantic, and workflow dimensions.

### Cross-System Practitioner

A general descriptor for anyone who works across multiple systems as a regular part of their role.

**Relationship to Systems Weaver:** Descriptive and accurate but lacks the craft connotation. Useful as a plain-language alternative when "Systems Weaver" requires explanation.

## Informal labels from wider usage

These terms are commonly applied to the kind of practitioner the guild serves. They are listed here for recognition, not endorsement.

- **Glue person** — someone who holds things together. Accurate but implies the work is supplementary rather than core.
- **Swiss army knife** — someone with many tools. Emphasises versatility but not the craft of connection.
- **Jack of all trades** — someone with broad skills. Often followed by "master of none", which misrepresents the depth of cross-boundary expertise.
- **Firefighter** — someone who responds to crises. Describes one mode of the work but not the proactive, structural practice.
